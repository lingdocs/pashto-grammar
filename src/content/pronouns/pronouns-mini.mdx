Mini pronouns are **enclitic prounouns** that hang on to the back of words. They can't be used at the beginning of a sentence. You can think of them as little babies ğŸ‘¶ that can't stand on their own. **They can't be used to start a sentence.** They always need to lean up against the back of another word.

## Where they're used

Mini pronouns can only be used as one of three things:

1. The **object** of a **present transitive** verb
2. The **subject** of a **past transitive** verb
3. A **possesive** pronoun

Below are some examples of how mini pronouns can be used in the place of regular pronouns. The mini pronouns are used when you don't need a strong emphasis on the pronoun.

## Other Notes

### Don't confuse with other <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ø¯Û", f: "de" }} />'s

The mini-pronoun <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ø¯Û", f: "de" }} /> is not to be confused with two other particles that look and sound the exact same:

 - The jussive <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ø¯Û", f: "de", e: "should, must" }} />
 - The demonstrative pronoun <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ø¯Û", f: "de", e: "this" }} />

### Mini-pronouns are used an order

Sometimes **you might see two mini pronouns right next to each other** because one is being used as a possesive and the other is being used for a subject or object. When this happens, the pronouns are ordered by person: 1st, 2nd, then 3rd.
