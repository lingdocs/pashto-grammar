In the <Link to="/inflection/inflection-intro/">previous chapter</Link> we talked about **why** words inflect. Now we will explain **how** words inflect. What actualy happens to them? How do they change?

Like many things in Pashto, inflection can seem mysterious and intimidating. Why do the words change so much, and how!? ğŸ˜© But, we'll see that there are just a few basic patterns that the words follow, and it's not so scary or confusing after all.

Below are the **6 basic patterns for inflecting** nouns and adjectives. Notice how all these patterns work with both masculine and feminine genders.

Don't worry about memorizing them all perfectly to start. Instead keep looking back and use them as guides to help you as you get familiar with the inflections over time.

## Not all words inflect

It's important to know that *not all words can be inflected*. If a word doesn't fit into one of these 6 patterns, you can't inflect it. For example, if an adjective ends in an <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ù€Ù‡", f: "-a" }} /> in the masculine form, you can't inflect it because it doesn't fit any of the patterns above. ğŸ™…â€â™‚ï¸

## Exceptions

Whatever kind of word you're using, it will always inflect for the <Link to="/inflection/inflection-intro/">three reasons</Link> that we mentioned earlier. However, there are two big exceptions. 

### Pattern 1 words in sandwiches starting with Ù¾Ù‡ - pu

When you put a <Link to="/inflection/inflection-patterns/#1-basic">pattern 1 - basic</Link> word in a the <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ù¾Ù‡ ... Ú©Û", f: "pu ... ke", e: "in" }} /> sandwich, **it will not use the first inflection**.
