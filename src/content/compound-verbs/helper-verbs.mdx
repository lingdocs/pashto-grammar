Pashto uses a number of helper verbs (auxillary verbs) to form compounds.

The most important ones are these four:

- Intransitive
  - <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©€êÿØŸÑ", f: "ked√∫l", e: "to happen" }} />
  - <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©€êÿØŸÑ", f: "ked√∫l", e: "to become ___" }} />
- Transitive
  - <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©ŸàŸÑ", f: "kaw√∫l", e: "to do ___" }} />
  - <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©ŸàŸÑ", f: "kaw√∫l", e: "to make ___ ___" }} />

Wait, how are these _four_ verbs, and not _two_?? ü§îü§î Don't the pairs look exactly the same? When you look at this you may feel like this lady does in this video.

<VideoPlayer url="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5YG6tlxv4eU" />

Yes, there really are four separate verbs. You're not seeing double, and this is not a trick. The verbs behave differently and have different meanings. And once we see them in the perfective form we will see that they really do look different too.

Think of these four verbs as four strange friends that you will need to get to know very well. They seem kind of weird at first, but once you get to know them, you won't be able to live without them.

## ⁄©€êÿØŸÑ vs. ⁄©€êÿØŸÑ

There's a big difference between <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©€êÿØŸÑ", f: "ked√∫l", e: "to happen" }} /> and <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "⁄©€êÿØŸÑ", f: "ked√∫l", e: "to become" }} />!
