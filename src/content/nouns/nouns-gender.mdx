All nouns in Pashto are either <Masc /> or <Fem />. Thankfully, you can pretty much always tell the gender of a word by its ending.

## Gender by Ending

Basically:

 - if a noun ends in a *consonant* (including ÛŒ - y at the end), it's <Masc />
 - if a noun ends in a *vowel*, it's <Fem />

## Exceptions

### Feminine words that lost their <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{ p: "Ù‡", f: "a" }} />

Some <Fem /> words have had their <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù‡", f:"a"}} />'s on the end chopped off. This makes them look like masculine nouns, but actually the do behave (and get inflected) just like other feminine nouns.

For example, the word <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù„Ø§Ø±", f:"laar", e:"road/way"}} /> ends in a consanant, but it's feminine. ðŸ¤” Why? It's just that the <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù‡", f:"a"}} /> on the end is usually left out. For the singular/plain form the word, you will hear people say either <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù„Ø§Ø±", f:"laar" }} /> or <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù„Ø§Ø±Ù‡", f:"laara" }} />, sometimes with or without the <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù‡", f:"a"}} /> on the end. But regardless, the word is always considered <Fem />, and it inflects just like any other feminine word ending with an <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù‡", f:"a"}} />. (eg. <InlinePs opts={opts} ps={{p:"Ù„Ø§Ø±Û", f:"laare", e: "roads" }} />)
